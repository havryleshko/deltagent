I measure a variance commentary first by how quickly it delivers the "so what" — can I scan the first page or two and immediately know which numbers matter, why they moved, and what I need to worry about or act on? If I have to hunt for the big swings or wade through boilerplate like "revenue was slightly above budget due to higher volume," it fails the CEO test. Good ones surface the three-to-five material drivers (by P&L line or business unit) in the opening summary and tie every explanation back to a dollar impact I can trace to the financials.


Next I check for intellectual honesty and business grip. I want root-cause language that shows the writer actually understands the operations — not "headcount increased" but "we hired 12 engineers in Q4 to hit the new product launch, which is now running three weeks late and burning $180k extra in contractor overtime." Defensive hedging, vague phrases ("market conditions"), or missing the obvious cross-link (e.g., sales beat but gross margin cratered because of unapproved discounting) instantly downgrades it. I also flag whether the commentary flags forward risk or mitigation plans; pure rear-view mirror work tells me the finance team is playing historian, not partner.


Finally, I look at polish and efficiency. Clean formatting (consistent variance thresholds, actual/budget/prior in the same table, no typos), brevity (one page per major category max), and an objective tone that doesn't sugar-coat bad news. If it takes me longer than five minutes to absorb the story and decide what to probe in the next meeting, the commentary isn't doing its job. The best ones make me think the author could sit in my chair for a week and not miss a beat.
